Solar Electric Vehicle Team (SEVT) - My Contributions

Battery Management System (BMS)

Our headboard, the green PCB, serves as our BMS, and has two main roles: Monitoring the state of the car's electronics and controlling relays to supply power. It tracks the current being drawn by and supplied to the motors, solar panels, and charger. I helped test this functionality by using our I2C library and a DC load to draw/deliver current and [make sure the values the headboard was reading were correct]. [These values are important] because they allow us to emergency shut off the car in case currents get dangerously high.
The headboard is also responsible for controlling the precharge and high power relays that connect to the motors, solar panels, and charger, which I also [helped] test using the I/O pins and contactors on the circuit board.

Cellboards

We have 8 cellboards on our battery, each of which is responsible for measuring the voltage and temperature of one of the battery's zones. They also balance our battery so all the cells are at roughly equal voltages. I [helped] set up and debug the IsoSpi connection between the headboard and the cellboards, allowing them to communicate while the car was operational. We use the values from the cellboards in the headboard to emergency shut off the car if our battery becomes dangerously hot or has too much/too little voltage.

Motors

[For the motors], my role included configuring a wavesculptor motor controller to operate with a Mitsuba D3 motor, writing new code to control the motors using CAN messages from one of our PCBs, and installing the motors on the car. I also wrote code for receiving telemetry from the motors, which includes information about their velocities, temperatures, and error states.

Dashboard and Telemetry

Our current car has several pieces of hardware that were not included on Nimbus, including an extra motor, a charger, and new MPPTs, all of which required adding new messages to our CAN library. This brought the total to 20 messages the dashboard needed to read, but the AT90CAN, our microcontroller, can only tranceive 15. To get around this, we rerouted the CAN, sending messages to other boards and merging them before sending them to the dashboard, which allowed us to have complete telemetry regarding every system on the car.
On the right side of the dashboard, there is a blue module labeled Xbee. This is the device we use to wirelessly transmit our telemtry from Gemini to our strategy team, which uses this information to make decisions about target speeds and charging times. I [helped] set up and test the Xbees when the dashboard was originally being soldered.
On the left, there is another blue device, our GPS module, which I wrote and tested code for to allow the strategy team to track our location during the race.
